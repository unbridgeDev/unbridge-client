# Security and threat model

This page states what Unbridge protects, what it does not, and what you have to trust. It
errs toward disclosure. If something is a weakness, it is listed here.

## What is hidden

Against an observer reading the chain:

- **Membership.** The signer set and the threshold are never on-chain. Authorization is a zero-knowledge proof.
- **Amounts.** Notes are Poseidon commitments. The chain holds ciphertext, not values.
- **Linkage.** A deposit and a later withdrawal cannot be tied together. Withdrawals settle at fresh addresses, submitted by a relayer.
- **Vault balance.** There is no account whose lamports equal the vault's holdings.

## What is not hidden

Shielded pools have visible edges. These are properties, not bugs:

- **That activity happened.** The chain shows a deposit into the pool and, later, a withdrawal out of it. The pool is public; the graph inside it is not.
- **Deposit denomination.** Deposits use fixed standard amounts. The denomination is visible. This is deliberate: uniform amounts are what make one deposit indistinguishable from another. Arbitrary amounts would be fingerprints.
- **The recipient, to the relayer.** Whoever submits a withdrawal and pays its fee sees the destination address at that moment. They do not learn who authorized it.
- **Anonymity set size.** Privacy is relative to how many indistinguishable deposits exist. A new pool gives little cover. It strengthens as the set grows.

## What you trust

- **The circuits and the proof system.** A bug in a circuit could let an invalid spend verify. The protocol is **unaudited**. Treat it accordingly.
- **The trusted setup, for now.** Groth16 needs a one-time setup. Phase one is the public Perpetual Powers of Tau. Phase two is circuit-specific and was bootstrapped by the project, so today you trust that the setup randomness was discarded. That assumption is being removed through an open ceremony: anyone can contribute entropy at [unbridge.dev/ceremony](https://unbridge.dev/ceremony), and one honest external contributor is enough to make the setup sound. Contributions are adopted in a scheduled, announced key rotation.
- **Your own device.** Proving and signing happen client-side. Your key share and the note secrets live on your machine. Losing them without a backup means losing access, though a vault can be recovered from chain data plus your wallet.

## What no one can do

By construction, not by promise:

- **No single party can spend.** A withdrawal needs a threshold of key shares. One member, the coordinator, and the relayer each cannot move funds.
- **The coordinator cannot sign.** It holds no shares. It relays ceremony messages and can be swapped out.
- **The admin cannot sweep the pool.** The program has no instruction that sends pool funds to an arbitrary address. Value leaves only via a valid proof consuming a note.
- **The relayer cannot alter a withdrawal.** The proof binds the exact outputs; changing the recipient or amount invalidates it.

## Known limitations we are working on

- **View key on the coordinator.** Team vaults store an encrypted view key with the coordinator so members can display shared balances. It can read amounts, not authorize spends. Distributing it to member keys directly (so the server never holds it) is planned.
- **Unaudited.** No third-party audit yet.
- **Ceremony in progress.** Until external contributions are adopted, the setup rests on operator honesty, disclosed above.
