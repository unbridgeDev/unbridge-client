Before writing a word, I went looking for the constraint to invert. The surface version of this seed is a six: "bridges are honeypots, so replace them with threshold signatures and hold native assets directly." NEAR's Chain Signatures already proved that category works. Restating it in Solana's accent would be a press release, not an insight. So the real question is what *specific* assumption Distin breaks that NEAR and the rest still hold. I think there is one, and it sits in an unglamorous place: where the signing protocol's coordination is allowed to live. Here is the article.

---

# Distin: when the custodian and the ledger become the same object

You hold Bitcoin, and you want to use it inside a Solana program. There is exactly one path open to you today, and it is a strange one. You send your real BTC to a contract, that contract locks it and mints you a token that says *someone owes you one Bitcoin*, and you carry that token around. The token is not Bitcoin. It is a claim against a contract, and its entire worth rests on that contract staying solvent and unbroken for as long as you hold it. Every headline bridge failure of the last few years was the same quiet event underneath: the claim kept circulating in people's wallets after the coin backing it had already walked out the door.

The usual way to explain this is that it's simply what crossing chains costs. Two ledgers that share no consensus cannot hand assets to each other directly, so something has to stand in the middle, hold the real thing, and vouch for the copy. Stated that way it sounds like physics. It isn't. It's the consequence of one decision: that the way to use an asset somewhere else is to *move the asset there*. Lock it, copy it, transport the copy. The pot of locked originals that this requires is, by construction, the largest single target on the network, and calling it a bridge instead of a vault doesn't change what it is.

## What actually has to cross

Look again at what you wanted. You wanted a Solana program to be able to spend your Bitcoin under conditions you set. The Bitcoin itself never needed to go anywhere. It can sit on Bitcoin, as native BTC, exactly where it is. The only thing that has to travel from Solana to the Bitcoin network is one signature over one transaction, at the moment you decide to spend.

So the real primitive was never transport. It was authority. If a set of signers can jointly produce a valid signature for a Bitcoin address, and no single one of them ever holds the whole key, then your BTC lives natively on Bitcoin and is *controlled* from Solana. No wrapped token exists. No vault of locked originals exists. There is nothing sitting in a contract to drain, because the asset was never taken into custody by a contract in the first place. Threshold signatures are how a group signs without any member holding the key, and FROST for the Schnorr and Ed25519 chains, threshold ECDSA for the secp256k1 chains like Bitcoin, Ethereum, and Tron, are the concrete machinery. This much NEAR already demonstrated, and I'm not going to pretend Distin invented the category. NEAR proved you can drive a native asset on one chain from an account on another. What it left open is the part that actually matters.

## The assumption no one questions: keep the coordination off-chain

Here is the constraint everyone in this space treats as settled, including the systems already shipping. A threshold signature is not produced in one shot. The signers run a protocol of several communication rounds: commitments first, then partial signatures, with timeouts and abort handling in between. The accepted wisdom is that this protocol runs **off-chain**, on a side network of MPC nodes gossiping among themselves, and the blockchain only ever sees the finished signature posted at the end. The chain is a bulletin board. The real work happens in a room you can't see into.

That choice was made for a sensible reason once: on-chain rounds were assumed to be too slow and too expensive to host an interactive protocol. But notice the price it quietly charges. If the coordination happens off-chain, then the economic security you bolt on, the staked tokens, the slashing, is watching the wrong thing. A signer can stall a round, equivocate, or send a malformed share, and the chain that's supposed to punish this never witnessed it. To slash for a fault in the signing protocol, you'd need to drag an off-chain transcript into an on-chain court and prove misbehavior after the fact. So in practice these systems slash for almost nothing that happens *inside* the protocol. The stake is a deposit with a sign on it, not a condition the ledger can actually enforce.

Distin's move is to refuse that split. Put the coordination on-chain. A user posts a signing intent as a Solana instruction. The signers post a partial as ordinary transactions, one per operator, each a public participation receipt. The program checks the threshold and records the off-chain aggregate signature for the foreign chain. Who took part, the deadline, and every missed cutoff are now ordered, public, timestamped facts on Solana, even though the cryptographic rounds run off-chain.

The thing that makes this possible rather than absurd is the one piece of the seed that isn't decoration: Solana's roughly 400ms slot. If a round is a slot, a two or three round protocol settles in something close to a second. On a twelve-second chain the same protocol takes most of a minute, which is unusable for interactive signing, and that is the actual reason a slow chain cannot be a control plane no matter how secure it is. Speed here isn't a feature you advertise. It's the gate that decides whether on-chain coordination is even a sentence you're allowed to say.

And publishing the rounds leaks nothing you needed to keep. The security of a threshold scheme lives in the secret shares, which never touch the chain. The transcript of commitments and partial signatures was always safe to make public, because the final signature is public anyway. So the off-chain room was never protecting a secret. It was just protecting itself from being watched.

When the coordination is on-chain, two things that used to be separate collapse into one. The safety of your Bitcoin and the consensus of the ledger watching the signers become the same object. Who participated and who missed the deadline isn't an accusation you have to prove; it's a row in Solana state. And when operators identify a cheater off-chain, a quorum signs a fault report the program verifies on-chain through Solana's Ed25519 program before it slashes, no admin in the loop. The stake stops being a sign and becomes a circuit. That is the inversion, and it's the difference between "MPC instead of bridges," which is a six, and a custody layer whose punishment can actually see the crime.

## Where this is weak, said plainly

A system that hides what you have to trust about it has already failed at the one job it exists for, so here is the trust, unsoftened.

The signer set is the floor. If a threshold of signers colludes, they sign your Bitcoin away, and slashing is a deterrent, not a wall. It raises the cost of theft to "more than the staked SOL and LST you can burn," which is real and measurable and still finite. Honest framing is that this buys you crypto-economic safety, not cryptographic impossibility, and those are not the same grade of promise.

Liveness cuts the other way. Because no one holds your whole key, no one, including you, can sign without a threshold of signers online and cooperating. You have traded the risk of a custodian running off for a dependency on a quorum showing up. That is usually the better trade, but it is a trade, not a free lunch.

The ECDSA chains are heavier than the prose makes them sound. Threshold Schnorr over Ed25519 is light and clean. Threshold ECDSA for Bitcoin and the EVM is more rounds and more cost, and that protocol family is still hardening in the literature rather than being a settled thing you bolt on without care. On-chain coordination makes that cost visible per signature rather than hiding it, which is the honest version and also the more expensive one.

And the sharpest weakness is the one the design is proudest of. One Solana account that signs for all your chains is also one pane of glass through which your entire cross-chain life is visible, every intent posted in the open by construction, and a single point whose compromise spends everything at once. The unification that removes the bridge also concentrates the blast radius and the surveillance. I'm not going to wave a privacy layer at this and call it solved, because nothing in what runs today solves it, and pretending otherwise would undo the only thing this kind of system is for.

## The one place the blast radius pays you back

There is, though, a turn inside that last weakness that is true now and not a promise. A raw Bitcoin key cannot have a policy. Bitcoin does not know what a daily limit is, or a timelock keyed to a second factor, or a co-signer required above some amount. The protocol simply signs what the key signs. But the instant a foreign asset's signing authority is mediated by a Solana program instead of a bare key, that program is the one place a policy can live, and it governs spends on chains that have no language to express a policy of their own. The same concentration that creates the blast radius is the first surface on which you can write one rule that holds across every chain at once. Authority piled into a single account is also governance piled into a single account, and one of those is a liability you watch and the other is a capability you couldn't have any other way. They are the same surface, read from two sides.

That is the whole of it. The asset stays home. Only the signature crosses. The coordination that produces it runs in the open on the one chain fast enough to host it, which turns the watcher and the custodian into a single object with nothing left inside to rob. What it costs you is named above and not rounded off.

A note on grade, since the brief asked for honesty about that too. The inversion is real: moving MPC coordination on-chain so that economic security finally has the participation, deadlines, and attestation-verified faults it's paid to punish on the same ledger is a genuine eight, and it's specifically what the NEAR-style incumbents leave on the table. The re-inversion of blast radius into a cross-chain policy surface holds it there. What keeps me from claiming higher is the surveillance pane, which I left open on purpose rather than patching with a future-tense paragraph. An honest 8 reads better than a dishonest 9.
